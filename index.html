INTERLEDGER

Payments across payment networks

Read the Whitepaper

The Problem

When paying online, depending on where you are in the world, you are either confronted with a barrage of payment options and a terrible UX, or you have no option at all.

The fundamental problem is that payment networks are not interoperable. The sender and receiver of a payment must both be on the same network. Few networks can reach global scale so there is little competition.

This situation is similar to the days before the Web. It took open standards to interconnect the early web services.

What about Bitcoin, Ripple and other so-called protocols for money? In reality, these are all just networks themselves.

We need a neutral protocol, like HTTP or SMTP, for payments on the Web that enables senders and receivers to each use different providers. It should not require a central operator, nor a globally accepted currency or blockchain. Anyone should be able to use the protocol without connecting to any core or official network.

How the Protocol Works

There are many payment systems or ledgers out there. Payment networks, blockchains, banks and so on.

Currency traders can create connections between them. You pay a trader on your ledger and the trader pays whoever you want on another ledger.

But the problem with this is: Who pays who first? If the trader goes first, they have to trust the sender to hold up their end of the bargain. If the sender goes first, the trader could disappear with the money, never to be seen again.

The solution is pretty simple: Escrow. Both the sender and the trader share a common ledger. And, the trader and receiver share another ledger. So let's have the sender’s ledger guarantee the transfer. The sender puts the money into escrow, the trader delivers funds at the destination, shows proof to the sender’s ledger and gets paid.

Using this protocol, anyone can transact across ledgers, without relying on a specific network.

Six Degrees of Separation

Trying to find currency traders for every possible pair of currencies or ledgers is unrealistic. The number of possible combinations explodes as the number of ledgers increases.

Instead, we can create chains of traders to enable payments between any sender and any receiver. Think six degrees of separation for money.

This means I can use whatever obscure payment system I want to pay you, even if you’re on a different one.

Benefits

Universal payments

Pay and receive payments using whatever means you prefer.

Competitive rates

Many traders compete to provide the cheapest, fastest payments.

Unlimited scalability

Without depending on a single system for processing payments, scalability becomes a non-issue. Need more speed? Just add more ledgers and more traders.
